01 · Context

A 2019 architecture asked to do 2022 work

By 2021, Vodacom's internal Engage app had two years of feature additions stacked on a 2019 architecture never designed to scale. The result: a 9-tile icon grid serving HR, IT, food menus, boardroom bookings and more — all at equal visual weight, with no search, no personalisation, and no awareness of who you were or what you needed.

I was brought in to fix more than the UI. I had to fix the mental model.

"I didn't pitch a visual refresh. I pitched an architectural rethink: replace the static grid with a system that surfaces the right thing to the right person at the right time."— Lulamile Mkhungela · Vodacom Group · 2021–2022

02 · Discovery

Problems identified

Three months of IT helpdesk tickets, employee interviews, and direct observation produced a concrete priority list stakeholders couldn't argue with:

01 · Hierarchy

No feature hierarchy

All 9 home tiles equal weight. Daily leave checks felt the same as monthly boardroom bookings.

02 · Personalisation

One home screen for everyone

Call centre agents and network engineers saw the same UI — no role, location, or behaviour awareness.

03 · Findability

No search

If you didn't know which of 9 categories held your answer, you explored — then raised an IT ticket.

04 · Visual system

Repetitive template

Red header + hero + icon grid everywhere. Content-heavy, action-driven, and time-sensitive sections all looked identical.

05 · Forms

Bare-bones form UX

No progress, no inline validation, no confirmation. Facilities and Gift & Hospitality drove disproportionate support contacts.

06 · Content

No contextual surfacing

Announcements, wellbeing, and rewards buried inside modules instead of on the home screen when time-sensitive.

03 · Principles

Four rules that governed every decision

  • Surface before search — answer "what does this person need right now?" with role, behaviour, and recency.
  • Personalisation earns trust — an app that knows your name and recent payslip feels like a tool, not a noticeboard.
  • Brand serves content — Vodafone Red as accent, not wallpaper. Dark charcoal surfaces reduced daily fatigue.
  • Forms are conversations — one decision at a time, context upfront, progress visible, completion celebrated.

05 · Process

Research first, test early (~4 months)

Phase 01

Discovery — tickets + interviews

Mapped top IT query categories to navigation failures. Spoke to 6 employees across engineering, HR, finance, and campus ops. Built an emotional journey from "opens app" to "gives up and calls IT."

Phase 02

IA redesign — card sort + tree test

Card sort with 12 employees challenged assumed groupings. Redesigned into 6 top-level categories plus search as navigation fallback.

Phase 03

Visual direction

Tested white/red refresh vs dark charcoal in parallel. Dark tested better for daily use and modern intent; selected and developed to hi-fi.

Phase 04

Wireframes → hi-fi in Figma

14 screens with auto-layout components so handoff was one living file, not static artboards.

Phase 05

Prototype testing — 2 rounds, 10 sessions

Round 1: navigation + profile. Round 2: COVID screening + Favourites. Search was most valued; avatar customisation drove highest positive sentiment.

Phase 06

Design system contribution

Contributed dark card variants, bottom nav, profile shell, avatar picker, Rewards4U banner, and multi-step COVID form pattern to the shared library.

#1Search & personalisation in feedback

Those weren't features added because they "sounded good" — they were the direct response to the two most common failure modes documented in discovery.

Before

Static 9-tile grid, no search, no personalisation — employees translated intent into org taxonomy every time, then called IT when they failed.

After

Card-based home, global search, favourites, dark daily-use system — navigation tickets dropped; search & personalisation ranked #1 in feedback.

My ownership

Lead UX/UI & product design: discovery, IA, visual direction, prototype testing, design-system contribution, annotated handoff.

08 · Lessons

What I still apply on enterprise redesigns

  1. Support tickets beat surveys — they show what people demonstrably can't do, not what they say they want.
  2. Self-directed personalisation gets used — My Favourites worked because employees curated it themselves.
  3. Own visual direction early — dark theme improved daily use but constrained photography and contrast; I documented and followed through on every implication.
