Product Design Process

Sefnet Portfolio

SEFNET is an app for book lovers that helps users track everything they own, books they have read, what they will read next, and also everything they have loved so far. In essence, it's quite similar to some reading books apps, with the key differentiation being the user-friendly, fresh, and clean design.

My Role:

UX / UI Designer

Process:

Research, interviews, survey, competitive analysis, ideation, sketching, prototyping, testing, and evaluation

Tools:

Figma

Challenge

SEFNET needs help to create a new user-centric app and establish a brand that will set them apart from the competition.

Design Challenge

SEFNET needs help to create a new user-centric app and establish a brand that will set them apart from the competition.

Design Question

How might we improve reading experiences, ensure that the users keep using the app and users are able to share their knowledge?

Goal And Objectives

Design an iOS app that serves the needs and wants of book lovers along with establishing a brand identity for SEFNET.

1. EMPATHIZE

Before starting off with the research, I outlined a detailed research plan including the main research goals, methods of research and timeline, in order to focus on achieving the goals of the research. My intention was to gather as much data as possible in such a small amount of time.

Research Findings

I conducted 5 semi-structured interviews with book readers and book writers combined and created a survey that got about 30 respondents. I aimed to understand the reading experience starting from the first interaction with the book selection to reading the book till the end.

Key Takeaways & Trends

Through the research process I created, I've been able to identify some specific motivations and pain points that bring insights into the mind of readers.

  • Most participants read 5+ books a year
  • Recommendations from friends/family is the most important factor when deciding what to read
  • Participants prefer to read physical books over e-books or audiobooks
  • Participants like to keep track of the books they read or want to read
  • Participants tend to NOT set reading goals. (Gamification Feature)
  • Participants do not like to rate/review books.
User Interview & Card Sorting

After the surveys I conducted, I was able to conduct 3 user interviews over the phone with participants from the survey. I asked participants additional follow-up questions about their past experiences with the apps they use on a regular basis.
I asked the participants to do an open card sorting exercise through OptimalSort. The cards sorted were a combination of keywords, sections, and categories from direct/indirect competitors. Insights from this study will be used to help design information architecture for the app.
The Similarity Matrix helped identify strong card pairings and potential groupings on a quick view. For each pair of cards, the intersecting cell shows the percentage of participants who grouped these cards together. The greater the score means the greater the pairing potential.

Key Findings

To understand how billing is currently being addressed, we looked at our direct and indirect competitors. None of them address the entire billing process or allow diners to pay to the restaurant.

  • Participants showed that they are used to the patterns of having a “Home” and some type of “Explore” option.
  • Participants expect to see their friends and social features under their “Profile” page.
Persona Development

After gathering the key findings from the research phase, I conceptualized a persona that would represent my ideal user's goals and needs.

2. DEFINE

Once the research phase was completed, I stepped into define phase with all of the data from primary and secondary research. The objective was to analyze and synthesize business goals along with user goals in order to asses which ideas would best suit for the needs of both.

Feature Roadmap

With the project goals in focus, I created a product feature roadmap, with features presented in order of priority in terms of development, investment, and order of importance to business and user goals.

3. PROTOTYPE

Using Figma, I started to create an interactive prototype that would give users scrolling ability throughout the app to navigate and to complete the tasks given. After outlining a testing plan consisting of three different tasks with a scenario each, I was ready to conduct testing.

4. TEST & ITERATIONS

I conducted an online moderated usability test with 5 participants. 2 of the participants read an average of 40+ books a year while the other read between 5-10 books a year. Getting these designs without brand in the hands of some potential users as soon as possible could ensure that the experience will be shaped and refined to truly meet customer needs and expectations. The testing tool was MURAL

Affinity Map

I created an affinity map based on feedback, observations, and notes from usability testing. I wanted to visually compile and synthesize the findings from each participant in one place showcasing the insights and results at specific screens of the app. I also added a section with the main takeaways from testing as well as the key iterations needed to be made before moving into incorporating UI.

Future Possible Features

  • Friends & Celebrities Recommendations
  • Friends' top 5 books
  • Ability to add libraries from other cities/states
  • Users that read 40+ books a year would like to see statistics on their reading habits

What I learned

- Move forward without knowing all the answers. Don't wait for permission, always be consciously curious and proceed forward with the best intentions. Keep thinking and pushing new ideas.

- Even though many of the questions have no answers, but asking these questions have allowed me to better understand the scope, identify the opportunities and make sure I'm not creating new problems.

- Keep your designs simple. Don't overload users with too much information. Define your critical user journeys early in the process and question every step or screen you create.

- Get feedback often and early in your design process. Don't be afraid to show incomplete or unfinished work to your team. Be humble, and try to understand others point of view.

Conclusion

In retrospect, I feel this exercise wasn't exactly what the client expected (probably an issue with my positioning) because I aimed towards thinking about the whole ecosystem instead of just the book reading app itself (and probably failed on communicating it sufficiently in the beginning).

  • Friends & Celebrities Recommendations
  • Friends' top 5 books
  • Ability to add libraries from other cities/states
  • Users that read 40+ books a year would like to see statistics on their reading habits

SmartServe Ordering System Portfolio

Have you ever dined at a restaurant and felt frustrated because of the waiting or figuring out how to split the bill? To address this problem, we created a SmartServe, a mobile app that improves the ordering, billing process by eliminating waiter interaction and allowing users to be able to split bills easily. In this project, I present a smartphone application which solves some of the major problems faced by customers and restaurant ordering management by integrating existing technologies and services to help people save time, money,comment on the food quality cooked by the Chef in a certain restaurant and create a memorable shopping experience whilst cutting number of waiter(ss) in a restaurant and helping a restaurants to cut cut costs/make 200% profits. SmartServe allows restaurant guests to place their order fast and efficiently on the table-side Tablet or their devices instead of waiting for a waiter to come to them. For the restaurants that do not have tableside Tablets installed; customers can use their devices. In those restaurants, SmartServe is integrated to beacon technology — a small device mounted in the restaurant to detect when someone enters the restaurant, and sends a notification to his or her phone. The moment guests walk in to the restaurant they get a notification on which they can click and the app will open in their browser, no installation needed.

My Role:

UX / UI Designer (screens or the Journey map, Persona, Research Insights, Sketching ideas & use cases,Defining the functionalities, Design decisions, low-fi/hi-fi Prototype isn't provided due to NDA)

Process:

Research, interviews, survey, competitive analysis, ideation, sketching, prototyping

Tools:

AdobeXd, Protopie and Miro

Design Challenge Question

How might we improve dining experiences at sit-down restaurants?

Goal And Objectives

I first created an empathy map then user journey for SmartServe to help me visualize the areas I needed to target for my research. With ordering system at hand by then(which includes :ordered item not available,delays in getting your order,no setting a date or time for the order you want,no option to be assisted when you not sure about what you want order) .

Rationale Behind SmartServe

Although we have all the technology at our disposal, the restaurants experience has not much advanced with the times. A lot of shoppers still face inconveniences in finding ordering online/ using apps when ordering, deciding which food to buy, getting discounted products & spending time in checkout lines only to find what you are ordering is not available or out of stock.

1. DISCOVERY

* On this phase I will focus on Competitor analysis, Understanding the customer, do User journeys and content auditing *
Identifying the problems:

The main goal of the research was to understand the goals, motivations and pain points of the user so that I could design a better solution for them.

Brainstorming:

Sketching ideas & use cases
After getting a good understanding of the problem space and the opportunities, I started brainstorming and sketching out different ideas/concepts to help our users.

Interviews:

I conducted semi-structured interviews with 10 regular customers for different restaurants experience to understand the problem space and their overall experience of ordering food from restaurants outlets/stores. This helped me to explore different problems & go deep down into few major issues that they currently face.

User Persona:(Understanding the user)

Based on our research data I created two different personas that represent the problems faced by our respondents.

Journey Mapping: (Analyzing behavior & experiences)

I created journey maps to get an in-depth knowledge about our users, their tasks, feelings, experiences and their mood at different points of time.

Market Research:

I studied some research. Some key results of this study showed that though restaurants outlets/stores are still using the old source for food ordering among all age groups, customers are using digital tools to order food more frequently.

Research insights By Framing the problem:(Understanding the user)

The major problems and needs are listed as follows:

  1. Users end up spending too much time in the restaurants outlets/store especially in things such as standing in checkout lines and finding what to order.
  2. Users want to see the reviews of food prepared by the Chef and compare prices.
  3. Users want to save money by finding food that are discounted or on special.
  4. Users want to know how much they are spending and compare food quality with price.
Integrating SmartServe:

When researching about existing solutions, I came across UberEats,MrD Foods and much more. SmartServe is a restaurant and retail concept that utilizes several technologies, including automated ordering,checkout and payment steps. Basically, you could simply order online using your smartphone, and get your food on your table,delivered at your location or collect it to the preferred pick-up points without the hassle of standing in checkout lines and paying to the cashier. I realized the solution was incredible, and wanted to expand upon it and not re-invent the wheel given that users usually face many other issues while ordering at the restaurants outlets/store.

2. CONCEPTING

* On this phase I will focus on Process flows, SiteMap, Low-,Mid,Hi fidelity wireframes *
User flow

After having identified persona, needs, the type of actions that will be wanted to be performed, and having understand the objective of the business behind the project, it is time for me to create a task analysis and user flows for the primary goals of our users.
Create User flows and Task analysis was helpful to avoid to put too much focus on individual pages, to make the design of the app more user-centered, and in understanding which pages were needed for the app before starting with the information architecture phase.

Site Map

The first development of the sitemap was created starting from the review of the user journeys and personas previously created.
The results of the first iteration of the sitemap were then verified and reworked with an open Card Sorting test with potential users.
The results of the card sorting showed a trend where the participants merged the cards in five main categories. With these results, the site-map was then refined as follow.

Low/Mid fidelity Wireframe

Once the Site-map was completed, and I knew precisely the screens that I need to create and their purpose, I started to draft digital mid and high fidelity wireframes.
I started to focus on the screens necessary to complete the users flow previously defined for the persona I have created.

3. PROTOTYPE AND USER TESTING

* On this phase I will focus on Prototyping, User Testing, Review *
Evaluations & walkthroughs

I conducted informal and formal cognitive walkthroughs. Informal testing was done in a controlled environment using paper prototypes & the formal testing was done with the AdobeXd prototype at a restaurant.

Informal Walkthroughs:

I conducted informal walkthroughs twice with our prototypes. The evaluators were some students at Joburg Center For Software Engineering along with Walter Sisulu University (Mthatha Campus) and were frequent online/in person restaurants customers. I printed out the paper prototypes and tested. Both the rounds provided us with some valuable feedbacks for further exploration and design.

Formal Walkthroughs:

I recruited three participants at a local restaurant, to test out high fidelity prototype. I asked each of them complete three tasks that utilizes primary features of the app and to think aloud the experience, thoughts or ideas regarding the prototype.

From this testing, I hoped to answer the following questions:

  • - Are the features of application useful when the users are ordering to different restaurants?
  • - Is this application easy to use? (Will the user know how to use this application?)
  • - Are there any changes to be made to improve this application?
    • Key Findings from Testing:

    Overall, the participants were very positive toward our prototype. Most of them did not have issues in completing tasks and appreciated the simplicity of design.One participant wanted to have a feature that allows to check other restaurants nearby that offers same item prices, so that the participant can decide whether they want to make a trip to that restaurant to save money. Furthermore, one participant pointed that the users may take someone's order when other person went to the lavetry tables in a restaurant when they are using this service.
    After all the test finished and received the insights I then make the ultimate decisions about what goes, and what needs to be reviewed/iterated then do the adjustments but before I did the adjustments I validated my design once again with Expoze.io tool

    Present Interactive Prototype To stackholders

    SUMMING UP

    The project of the SmartServe was for restaurant owners and end users, an excellent opportunity to explore in deep all the end-to-end Ux Design process from a User and business perspective.
    I understood the importance of specifying the context of the use of the application, who will use it, under what conditions they will use it, and to be able to define business requirements and user goals that need to be met for the success of the product. I realize how it can be useful to learn what works and what doesn't works from the competitors.
    I also particularly enjoyed performing user interviews and usability testing, where I was surprised to discover how each user can react so differently when trying to solve their problems. This reinforced the concept of how helping the users to reach their goal easily always needs to be the main focus of my design.

    What I learned

    From this project, I had a valuable experience of testing with actual users in the actual environment this application is designed to be used. Although it was quite anxious at first to ask someone I do not know for a participation, at the end of the day, I learned that people were quite willing to add their insights about current technologies used and the improvements of the current technologies to latest ones regarding the iterative process. I realized that the design can look very different depending on the medium it is being presented. When I used the paper prototype, none of the participants had any problem with font size, because the design was printed on A4 size papers. But when I moved the design to a cell phone screen, the participants started to point issues with font size.

    Limitations

    The biggest limitation of our project would be that the AR technology was not implemented in our prototype. Therefore, I had to ask the participants to act as if the prototype had the AR feature. If our prototype could have incorporated the technology, it would have led to more discoveries on potential problems our prototype may have. Another limitation is that our participants for testing were mostly young adults. I do have results from elders, who may be less familiar with AR technology or online ordering system than the youth participants I tested the prototype with.

    iFly Portfolio

    This project I did at the Hackathon and we chose the flight booking challenge as we saw there is a need to improve customer and user experience from the current system by then back in 2018 as a team.

    My Role:

    UX / UI Designer

    Process:

    Research, interviews, survey, competitive analysis, ideation, sketching, prototyping, testing, and evaluation

    Tools:

    Zoom, JustInMind, AdobeXD,Marker, SurveyMonkey, ScreenFlow

    Challenge

    Conduct research on existing flight booking websites and use findings to design a user-friendly flight booking web application.

    1. RESEARCH

    Testing

    To kick off the project, I started with some usability testing for the purpose of gaining some deep qualitative insights into the usability of existing flight-booking apps. The first step was to define my objectives and to decide exactly what I wanted to achieve from the usability tests. I created a recruitment screener, a consent form and a script for both mobile and desktop usability testing sessions. Careful attention was paid during the writing of the scripts to ensure that I didn't ask leading questions or closed questions that would be unfruitful. After doing this preliminary work, I conducted two mobile and two desktop usability tests on the SAA Airways and Mango Airways websites.

    Objectives

    • Identify typical user behavior on the SAA Airways and Mango Airways websites.
    • Discover the primary goals of the user.
    • Discover which features conform to the users expectations.
    • Discover which features do not conform to the expectations of the user.
    • Identify elements that cause friction.
    • Learn what kinds of problems will likely cause the user to leave the website.
    • Discover where and when users feel empowered and frustrated during the journey.
    • Identify which pieces of information are important for the user.

    Depth Interviews

    The next step was to prepare a script for depth interviews for the purpose of gaining some insights into context of use. The aim was to understand which flight-booking websites users prefer and why. Scope also included how many times users typically return to a website before making a purchase and how users share information with one another when choosing flights as a group.

    Competitor Benchmarking

    On top of the usability testing and depth interviews, I wanted to draw some learning from best-in-class competitor websites and apps. I researched four flight-booking websites, and four mobile flight-booking apps by taking screenshots from each stage of the journey and assessing what had been done well, what had been done badly and which things are standard features that users expect.

    Online Survey

    I created an online survey using Survey Monkey for some more quantitative-attitudinal research to gain more understanding into context of use. I used a mixture of open and closed questions as well as some multiple choice questions. The survey would take around 2-3 minutes for people to complete.

    2. ANALYSIS

    Affinity Diagram

    Next, it was time to start making sense of the data. Firstly, I watched the usability test and took notes. I then wrote each point down. I also went through the the depth interviews, competitor benchmarking document and survey results. I got stuck in a room and sorting them into categories to create an affinity diagram. Some clear categories emerged

    Customer Journey Map

    I took the information from the affinity diagram and organized it into a step by step view of the journey to show how the users felt at each stage. I also noted their goals, behavior, context and pain points at each step.

    Empathy Map

    Finally, I created an empathy map showing the user's goals along with what they say, think, feel and do, since sometime what people say isn't always reflected by what they do. This helped me to empathize with the user, to gain a greater understanding of context of use, and to add to my background information of typical users.

    Key Findings

    The three key insights I gained by using these research methods were a) that error messages can be very unhelpful when it comes to informing the user about problems- how it can be avoided / resolved and what to do next, b) that users typically skip the add-ons section and c) that users prefer to check out as a guest rather than creating an account. Focus on these areas could prevent a lot of friction.

    3. DESIGN

    Mobile Flow Chart

    Using the finding from my research, I was able to create a mobile flowchart aimed at mitigating some of the issues that I discovered. Given that there is a large number of ways that a user could use a flight-booking site, I decided to narrow my scope to a linear primary use case. This was partly because all four of my usability test candidates skipped the “add-ons” and “create account” sections. Moreover, flight booking journeys tend to be very linear with later steps depending on earlier steps.

    Desktop Flow Chart

    I took the information from the affinity diagram and organized it into a step by step view of the journey to show how the users felt at each stage. I also noted their goals, behavior, context and pain points at each step.

    Define Navigation

    Here are some sketches I did while thinking through the navigation. On desktop, the idea was to have a primary and a secondary nav bar that are displayed at all times, except when the user is in the “checkout” part of the journey, at which point, the nav bar would be hidden to remove unnecessary distractions. In the place of the nav bar, the user will see a “back” button and a progress bar, to indicate how much is left to complete. On mobile, the primary navigation will be off-canvas and will only be visible when the menu icon has been tapped. However, secondary navigation icons will be visible until the user enters the checkout. The decision was made to use an of-canvas navigation on mobile because the primary purpose of the website is to book flights, so other areas of the website are seen as secondary use cases.

    Mobile Interaction Design

    The next step in the design process was to decide how each element on the page would work when the user interacts with it. I wanted to make the forms on each page as easy to complete as possible so I ensured that the appropriate keyboard would be displayed for each input field.

    Desktop Interaction Design

    Another thing that could help the user fill out the search form is to suggest recent searches when the input field is first selected. Also, when three or more characters have been entered, the user will see alphabetical suggestions in a drop-down list.

    Wireframe

    After completing the research, analysis, design and prototyping tasks documentation could be collated for handing over to the development team. Ideally, everything should have been considered by this point so that the dev team don't need to guess or fill in any blanks.

    4. TEST & ITERATIONS

    Key Findings From Testing

    Overall, the participants were very positive toward the flow. Most of them did not have issues in completing tasks and appreciated the simplicity of the booking process in the design.
    One user wanted the product to have an ability to see the cheapest flights fare first so that they can make a decision quick and save money. Further more one participant was happy with the find flights page as it shows flights prices from cheap to expensive with dates and search results for different flights companies

    Learnings

    During this project I learned a number of things but the most important of all was how to conduct a usability test,to manage time not to relax because of the given deadline as I could not full design and interactive design of my solution,and adaptation in a transmuted deadline. I am pretty sure that my design instructions were solving real user problems. Going forward, I feel confident about preparing for, conducting and analyzing the output from usability tests.

    What I Enjoyed

    The thing I enjoyed the most was how I collaborated with the Lecturer and other students through the UX process. It was great to work with team members feeling united while working towards a common goal. I also enjoyed gaining insights into real user behavior so that I could build more empathy towards the user and fully appreciate their needs
