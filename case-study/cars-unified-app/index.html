THE OLD DESIGN

The original Engage app launched in 2019 and served Vodacom employees well — for a while. By 2021, feature-by-feature growth had turned a clean tool into a navigation maze.

A call centre agent in Johannesburg and a network engineer in Cape Town saw the same 9-tile home screen. Every module — HR, Food Menu, AskIT, Boardroom, Gift & Hospitality — used identical red-header + hero image + icon grid templates, regardless of content type. There was no search, no personalisation, no hierarchy between daily tasks and once-a-year actions. Employees were raising IT helpdesk tickets for things the app could already do — they simply couldn't find them.

WHAT CHANGED

This was an architectural rethink — not a visual refresh. Every decision mapped to a specific problem surfaced in discovery: 3 months of IT helpdesk ticket analysis, interviews with 6 employees, and a card sort with 12 participants. Below is the decision log.

(03)
THE NEW DESIGN

The redesigned Engage moved from a static grid to a contextual, card-based experience built around real employee workflows. The home screen leads with a personalised greeting, Rewards4U badge, and a curated card stack showing what's new, relevant, and recently accessed — the only screen most employees ever need to visit.

• My Favourites — user-curated shortcuts, configured once, persisted across sessions — no admin required

• Navigate Engage overlay — full-screen sheet for deep navigation, replacing the 9-tile grid without cluttering home

• Rewards4U first-class — dedicated bottom nav tab and home screen banner; no longer buried in HR

• Profile panel — slide-in sheet with name, title, department, employee number; language switcher in one tap; illustrated avatar selector

• Dark charcoal visual language — reduces daily eye fatigue; brand expressed through red accents and the Vodafone swoosh

(04)
THE RESULTS

A redesign that earned its keep. The IT support team confirmed a meaningful reduction in navigation-related helpdesk tickets post-launch — exactly the pain point the project was built to address. Employee feedback highlighted search and personalisation as the highest-value additions. Avatar customisation drove the highest positive sentiment in testing — a small touch that made the app feel like it actually belonged to the person using it.

Vodacom Design System

Libraries in Adobe XD, documented in InVision DSM (tokens, components, governance), with Storybook as the in-code reference — dark & light tokens, card variants, bottom nav, profile shell, avatar picker, Rewards4U banner, and the COVID multi-step flow were threaded through that toolchain during the Engage programme.

XD → DSM → Storybook (diagram, not a product render)

Why this stack: XD held the working UI; DSM locked tokens and component names for hand-off; Storybook kept props and states honest in code — less drift than static exports alone.
